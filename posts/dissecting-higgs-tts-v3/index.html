Where Higgs TTS v3 learns who is speaking — layer by layer.Higgs TTS v3는 몇 번째 레이어에서 '누가 말하는지'를 알아차릴까 — 레이어별 지도.

Higgs TTS v3 sorts out who you are almost at the door, assembles what you speak mid-stack — and never quite finishes deciding how you feel, unless you tell it. We probed all 36 layers of the 4-billion-parameter voice cloner while it spoke: gender and language are essentially solved by the middle of the network (≈99% cross-validated), but emotion heard from an actual voice averages only 62% at its best layer — while the model's own one-token emotion control reads at 99.5% on a completely neutral voice, and at a flat 100% across actors the probe has never seen. We call this depth ordering — who, then what, then how — the identity ladder.Higgs TTS v3는 당신이 누구인지를 거의 입구에서 분류하고, 무엇을 말하는지를 스택 중반에서 조립한다 — 그런데 어떤 감정인지는 끝내 다 결정하지 못한다. 말해주지 않는 한. 이 40억 파라미터 음성 복제 모델이 말하는 동안 36개 레이어 전부에 선형 프로브를 대 보았다: 성별과 언어는 네트워크 중반이면 사실상 해결(교차검증 ≈99%)되지만, 실제 목소리에서 들은 감정은 최고 레이어에서도 평균 62%에 그친다 — 반면 모델 고유의 한-토큰 감정 제어는 완전히 중립적인 목소리 위에서 99.5%로, 프로브가 한 번도 본 적 없는 배우들에 대해서는 100%로 읽힌다. 이 깊이 순서 — 누구, 그다음 무엇, 그다음 어떻게 — 를 우리는 '정체성의 사다리'라고 부른다.

Method, in sixty seconds방법 요약, 60초

How do you read a talking model?말하는 모델을 어떻게 '읽는가'?

Higgs-Audio v3 is an autoregressive audio LLM: a 36-layer, 4B-parameter Qwen3 backbone that takes a reference voice plus a transcript and speaks. While it generates, we attach read-only hooks and save one mean residual-stream vector (2,560 dims) per layer per clip. That's the entire instrument.Higgs-Audio v3는 자기회귀 오디오 LLM이다. 36층·40억 파라미터 Qwen3 백본이 참조 목소리와 대본을 받아 말한다. 생성되는 동안 읽기 전용 hook을 붙여 클립마다 레이어별 평균 residual-stream 벡터(2,560차원) 하나씩을 저장한다. 도구는 그게 전부다.

On top of those vectors we ask two questions per attribute, per layer: can a held-out linear probe decode it? (readability — the classic instrument of Alain & Bengio, applied layer-wise in the speech-encoder tradition, here during actual voice-cloning generation) and how far apart are the category clusters? (geometry — Fisher separation and discriminant η²). Five attribute lenses: six FLEURS languages, male/female voices, four VCTK English accents, and five RAVDESS emotions probed two ways — the emotional reference recording alone, or the same recording with the model's own single-token control <|emotion:…|> prepended (drawn from the 21-token emotion vocabulary the model ships) — plus a third emotion condition: the token on a genuinely neutral carrier voice (told-only); fearful and disgust extend the convergence analyses. The statistics are conservative: every probe number is a mean over 100–300 repeated stratified splits with 95% bands; every axis carries a label-shuffled permutation null; accent gets a leave-one-speaker-out test; every axis gets a model-free acoustic-feature baseline on its actual input audio; and the probe choice itself is stress-tested (ridge λ is irrelevant, PCA k∈{{64,128}} agree within 0.2 points, and a probe-free nearest-centroid classifier preserves the ladder's order). Everything ran on one laptop (M1 Max, MPS).그 벡터 위에서 속성마다·레이어마다 두 가지를 묻는다. held-out 선형 프로브가 해독할 수 있는가?(가독성 — Alain & Bengio의 고전적 도구를 음성 인코더 전통처럼 레이어별로, 여기서는 실제 음성 복제 생성 중에 적용) 그리고 범주 군집이 얼마나 떨어져 있는가?(기하 — Fisher 분리도와 판별 η²). 렌즈는 다섯 개: FLEURS 6개 언어, 남/여 목소리, VCTK 영어 억양 4종, 그리고 RAVDESS 감정 5종을 두 방식으로 — 감정이 실린 참조 녹음 단독, 또는 같은 녹음에 모델 고유의 단일 토큰 제어 <|emotion:…|>를 앞에 붙여서(모델이 탑재한 21개 감정 토큰 어휘에서 가져옴) — 그리고 세 번째 감정 조건: 진짜 중립 캐리어 목소리 위의 토큰(told-only); fearful과 disgust는 수렴 분석까지 확장된다. 통계는 보수적으로 잡았다: 모든 프로브 수치는 100–300회 반복 층화 분할의 평균 + 95% 밴드이고, 모든 축에 라벨 셔플 순열 귀무가 붙으며, 억양에는 화자 분리(leave-one-speaker-out) 검정, 모든 축에 실제 입력 오디오에 대한 모델-프리 음향 특징 베이스라인이 추가되었고, 프로브 선택 자체를 스트레스 테스트했다(ridge λ 무관, PCA k∈{{64,128}} 0.2포인트 이내 일치, 프로브 없는 최근접-중심 분류기도 사다리의 순서를 보존). 전부 노트북 한 대(M1 Max, MPS)에서 돌았다.

Same five emotions, same speakers, three conditions. Voice only: the model hears the emotional reference. Voice + token: it hears the same recording and additionally gets the purpose-built control token (one dedicated vocabulary entry, out of the 21 emotions it ships tokens for). Token only — the token rides a genuinely neutral carrier voice. Cross-validated, the split is stark: hearing alone averages 62% at its best layer (95% band 47–77%). Two handles, one coarse latent두 손잡이, 하나의 거친 잠재 방향

Telling the model an emotion and letting it hear one move the network in overlapping directions — but the shared code is coarse: essentially one “emotional-expressiveness” direction, only weakly emotion-specific.감정을 말해주는 것과 들려주는 것은 네트워크를 겹치는 방향으로 움직인다 — 그러나 공유된 코드는 거칠다: 본질적으로 하나의 “감정 표현성” 방향이고, 감정 특이성은 약하다.

Here is the geometry beneath that gap. The told-only capture gives token-on-a-neutral-voice directions (vtold = mean(emotion) − mean(neutral)) that share no audio with the heard-emotion directions (vvoice) — and they converge: angry 0.902 (L6), disgust 0.878, happy 0.863, surprise 0.859, fearful 0.765 (six emotions). But a big cosine deserves three questions before it means anything. What's the floor? Random same-size contrasts from these very datasets give |cos| ≈ 0.23 (the anisotropy-matched null — 14× the naive random-vector floor of 0.016). Is it emotion-specific? Only modestly: the full voice×told matrix has happy-voice aligning with angry-told at 0.926 — above happy's own diagonal (0.863); the six-emotion diagonal beats the off-diagonal 0.78 vs 0.74 at layer 6. Is some of it a shared confound? Yes: our emotional draws are 6M/14F against neutral's 11M/9F, and gender is the model's most readable direction — projecting gender out drops angry from 0.902 to 0.73. The deepest version: the six heard-emotion directions are themselves mutually aligned at 0.72–0.96 at layer 6 — the layer-6 “emotion code” is essentially one emotional-expressiveness direction with small per-emotion tilts, and the per-emotion identity the probes read at 99.5% lives in that much smaller component. Perpendicular by design설계된 직교성

The flashiest number in this dataset is a 100% accent probe from layer 11 onward (US · British · Indian · Irish) — and it is wrong in an instructive way. Read the paper — Dissecting Higgs TTS v3: Layer-Resolved Linear Readability of Speaker Attributes in a Voice-Cloning Audio LLM

Dissecting Higgs TTS v3: Layer-Resolved Linear Readability of Speaker Attributes in a Voice-Cloning Audio LLM

Jaewon · Independent researcher · lonj7798@gmail.com

Abstract. We ask where a voice-cloning audio language model represents the attributes of the voice it is cloning. During generation by a 4B-parameter, 36-layer autoregressive TTS model (Higgs-Audio v3), we capture one mean residual-stream vector per layer per clip with read-only hooks, and slide held-out linear probes up the stack for five attribute views (languages, gender, accents, and emotion heard from a reference recording vs. told via the model's native control token). Attributes become linearly readable in a consistent depth order — told emotion and gender near-ceiling mid-stack, language 98.9% at layer 19 — while emotion heard from an emotional recording averages only 61.7% at its best layer, completing a hear/tell 2×2 (chance 20% / 61.7% / 98.3% / 99.5%) that survives actor-disjoint evaluation. With fully independent audio, told-only and voice-only directions converge (raw cos up to 0.90) — but calibration we argue should be standard for such claims (split-half reliability ceilings, cross-emotion specificity, anisotropy-matched nulls, sex-composition controls) shows the shared latent is coarse: off-diagonal alignment reaches 0.93, the matched null is 0.23, and the previously headlined "sad exception" (0.445) dissolves under reliability correction. Language and gender directions are near-orthogonal at every layer, consistent with compositional cloning; a language probe transfers across corpora; and a leave-one-speaker-out test converts our flashiest number — a 100% accent probe — into a measured diagnostic (collapse to 67.7%, restoring genuine mid-stack structure). Acoustic-feature baselines separate what the model computes from what the stimulus already contains. All measurements are observational — read-only maps of what layers encode, not claims about causal use.

1 Introduction

A voice-cloning text-to-speech (TTS) model receives a few seconds of reference audio and a transcript, and must implicitly answer a stack of questions before emitting its first audio token: what language is this? who is speaking? are they male or female? how do they feel? Modern voice cloners answer these questions well enough to speak almost any language in almost any voice, but where in the network those answers live — and whether attributes specified through different interfaces share representations — has received far less attention than the analogous questions for text LLMs [1, 2] and self-supervised speech encoders [3].

We map the residual stream of Higgs-Audio v3, a 4B-parameter autoregressive audio LLM with a 36-layer Qwen3-style backbone [4], while it performs real voice-cloning synthesis. The instrument is deliberately minimal: one mean residual vector per layer per generated clip, read-only forward hooks, then held-out linear probes and discriminant geometry on top. This yields a layer-resolved map of five attribute views — language, gender, accent, and emotion with and without the model's own control token — that we summarize as an identity ladder (Figure A1).

Our contributions:

  • A depth-ordered readability map, cross-validated (§4.1). Speaker attributes become linearly decodable at characteristic, ordered depths — token-conditioned emotion ≈95% by layer 11 and at ceiling mid-stack, gender and language mid-stack — with 95% bands over 100–300 repeated splits and label-shuffled permutation nulls; emotion inferred from an emotional recording averages 61.7% [47–77] at its best layer and never saturates.
  • A hear/tell 2×2 for emotion, and a calibrated convergence result (§4.2). Hearing alone: 61.7%. Hearing + token (audio held constant): 98.3%. Token on a neutral carrier (told-only, corrected capture): 99.5%, and 100% under speaker-disjoint CV — emotional audio adds nothing to a clean instruction. With fully independent audio the two routes' directions converge (raw cos 0.76–0.90 over five emotions at layer 6) — but calibration shows the convergence is coarse: the cross-emotion off-diagonal is nearly as high (0.68 vs. 0.77), an anisotropy-matched null sits at 0.23, part of the raw value is a shared sex-composition component, and the previously headlined "sad exception" (0.445) dissolves under split-half reliability correction — sad's direction is weak, not misaligned.
  • Geometry consistent with compositionality, at every layer (§4.3). Language and gender directions are near-orthogonal across all 36 layers (mean |cos| ∈ [0.113, 0.215], raw vectors; norm retention ≥ 97.0% after projecting gender out). Mid-stack (layers 8–28) the worst pair of the six language directions peaks at |cos| ≈ 0.51, before an ≈13–19× norm explosion (layer 34 vs. layer 12) reorganizes the geometry in the deepest layers.
  • A measured diagnostic for probe artifacts (§4.4). Our flashiest number — a 100% accent probe from layer 11 onward, flagged by its flat layer profile and 3-speakers-per-accent provenance — is now tested: under leave-one-speaker-out evaluation it collapses to 67.7% (chance 25%) at layer 20, and the collapse restores the rising-then-peaking layer structure of a genuine content attribute. Speaker-disjoint evaluation is standard in paralinguistics [5]; we re-issue it for layer-resolved probing, paired with a shape diagnostic (a flat layer profile as the tell) specific to that setting.

Everything here is a map of what layers encode, not a claim about what the model causally uses; intervention-based analysis of the same model is reported elsewhere and deliberately excluded here.

2 Related Work

Probing. Linear probes are the standard instrument for asking what a representation makes linearly explicit [1], with well-known caveats along three axes: probe capacity and dataset artifacts [2, 6], information-vs-extractability trade-offs [7, 8], and the gap between what a layer encodes and what the model uses [9]. Our deliberately low-capacity PCA(64)+ridge probe with label-shuffled permutation nulls follows the spirit of this literature: the null bounds what the fixed pipeline extracts from structureless labels, complementary to control-task selectivity [6].

Layer-wise analysis of speech models. A substantial encoder tradition maps where speech attributes live by depth: layer-wise probing of wav2vec-style models [3, 10], SUPERB layer-weight analyses [11], WavLM's speaker-vs-content layer findings [12], language identity in SSL representations [13], and layer-resolved speech emotion recognition [14]. The speaker-early/content-mid ordering we observe in a generative decoder echoes what those studies report for SSL encoders; what is new here is (i) capture during autoregressive voice-cloning synthesis in a codec-token TTS model — to our knowledge the first layer-resolved probing of a production voice cloner during generation — (ii) the token-vs-voice conditioning routes, and (iii) the depth-resolved direction geometry.

Linear representations and cross-route convergence. The view that high-level features occupy linear directions in the residual stream underlies much recent interpretability work [15, 16], including cross-model convergence [17]. The closest analogues of our two-interface result are multimodal neurons [18] — concept-level convergence across input modalities — and function/task vectors [19, 20], where instructions and demonstrations induce coinciding representations in text LLMs. Our result is an audio-generation analog: a control token and an acoustic exemplar writing overlapping residual directions, measured layer-by-layer with the calibration (reliability ceilings, cross-emotion specificity, matched nulls) that §4.2 shows is essential before reading such cosines at face value. The projection-out tool we use for cross-view geometry has its own pedigree in the gender-direction and nullspace-projection literature [21, 22]; multilingual direction geometry has likewise been mapped in text models [23].

Expressive TTS and attribute disentanglement. A separate engineering line builds the two interfaces we measure: style tokens and prosody transfer put "tell" and "hear" controls into TTS by construction [24, 25], AutoVC disentangles speaker from content through bottlenecks [26], and prompt-controlled TTS makes the "tell" interface natural language [27, 28]. Those works engineer separate interfaces or disentangled factors at training time; this paper measures, layer by layer, whether a production model's two native interfaces (control token vs. reference audio) converge internally — and finds convergence that is real but coarse (§4.2).

Audio LLMs. Codec-token language models now dominate zero-shot TTS [29]. Higgs-Audio v3 [30] conditions on a reference voice and transcript and generates interleaved audio tokens; its backbone exposes a standard residual stream, making text-LLM interpretability tooling directly applicable. Probing of audio models has concentrated on SSL encoders and, recently, audio-understanding LLMs; the generative, voice-cloning side is nearly untouched.

3 Setup

Model and capture. We run bosonai/higgs-audio-v3-tts-4b (36-layer Qwen3-style backbone [4], hidden size 2560) via its community transformers port, in bf16 on a single Apple M1 Max (MPS). During each voice-cloning generation we attach read-only forward hooks to all 36 decoder layers and store the sequence-mean residual-stream vector per layer, giving one 36×2560 summary per clip. Hooks are removed after capture; no weights or activations are modified anywhere in this study.

Attribute views and data. Five views over four corpora: (i) language: six FLEURS languages [31] (en, de, fr, ko, ja, zh), residuals captured during monolingual production synthesis (reused from a prior capture of the same model); (ii) gender: male vs. female FLEURS reference voices (English carrier for both groups), labels taken from the FLEURS metadata gender column; (iii) accent: four English accents (US, British, Indian, Irish) from a VCTK mirror [32] (mic1; 3 speakers per accent); (iv–v) emotion, two conditions: five RAVDESS emotions [33] (neutral, happy, angry, sad, surprise), probed as the emotional reference recording alone, or as the same recording with the model's native single-token control <|emotion:·|> prepended and a neutral reference transcript (20 clips per emotion per condition; the same 20 recordings underlie both) — an audio-held-constant ±token contrast, the result of a capture-code discovery narrated in §4.2. A corrected re-capture adds the told-only condition (token on a speaker-matched neutral carrier), and a second revision extends the emotion set with fearful and disgust (both single native tokens) in both conditions.

Notation. For clip i of a view, xi(ℓ) ∈ ℝ2560 is the sequence-mean residual at layer ℓ ∈ {0,…,35}. For class c under conditioning route r ("voice" = clone an emotional recording; "tag" = the model's control token), μrc(ℓ) is the class mean and vrc(ℓ) = μrc(ℓ) − μrneutral(ℓ) the class's shift vector (one-vs-rest diff-of-means for the non-emotion views).

Lenses. Per layer ℓ and view, on the mean residuals: (a) probe accuracy: a ridge classifier (one-hot least squares, λ=1) on PCA features — k = min(64, ntrain−1), so gender's effective k is 27–33 — trained on a derive split and evaluated held-out (language ntest=120; emotion n=30 per route; accent n=24; gender n=8); §4.5 shows the results are insensitive to k, λ, and probe family; (b) Fisher separation: between-class over within-class scatter along discriminant directions; (c) discriminant η²: in-sample separated-variance fraction after a regularized LDA in a 48-dim PCA subspace (used only for visualization — we read its shape across layers, not its absolute value); (d) route convergence (emotion only): cos(vtagE(ℓ), vvoiceE(ℓ)), reported with three calibrations the revision adds — split-half reliability of each side (the noise ceiling), the cross-emotion off-diagonal cos(vtagE′, vvoiceE) (the specificity control), and an anisotropy-matched null (random-subset diff-of-means pairs); (e) cross-view orthogonality: mean |cos| between the six language one-vs-rest mean-offset directions and the gender direction, plus the norm fraction each retains after projecting out the other's subspace (all 36 layers in the revision).

4 Results

4.1 The Identity Ladder

Figure A1 and Table A1 give the map. Reading it bottom-up:

Speaker-identity cues are available near the input. Gender is 87.7% readable at layer 0 (CV mean) and sits at or near ceiling from the mid-stack on (peak 99.2% [92–100]). The (confounded) accent probe likewise starts at 93.2% at layer 0. Whatever the network does with speaker identity, its linear traces are present early and consolidate mid-stack; gender's Fisher separation peaks at layer 24, deeper than language's (layer 19).

Content attributes assemble mid-stack. Language rises from 80.0% at layer 0 (CV mean) through a mid-stack plateau (peak 98.9% [97–100] at layer 19), and decays toward the output (89.2% at layer 35). Its Fisher separation peaks at layer 19 and collapses in the last two layers — the deepest layers appear to rotate toward output-token statistics rather than attribute structure (§4.3).

Felt emotion is the hardest listening task — at our n. Emotion inferred from an emotional reference recording starts at 38.4% (chance 20%) and averages only 61.7% [46.7–76.7] at its best layer (22) — our originally published 76.7% single split sat at the very top of that band, and the learning curve is still rising at our maximum training size, so 61.7% is a lower bound rather than a ceiling (§4.5). The same emotions, specified through the model's dedicated control token, are near-ceiling far earlier. That contrast is the subject of §4.2.

The ladder survives a corpus swap. A probe that only learned FLEURS recording artifacts would not survive new speakers, microphones, and speaking styles. It does: trained on all 600 FLEURS clips and tested on 870 in-the-wild Emilia clips of the same six languages (disjoint real speakers, podcast-style audio, captured under identical conditions in the prior sweep), the language probe reads 97.0% at layer 19 — the same peak layer, at nearly the within-corpus accuracy. Gender transfers even more sharply: the probe trained on 40 FLEURS clips reads the 80 VCTK clips (real speakers with official metadata genders) at 98.8%, and even the sign of projection onto the raw FLEURS diff-of-means direction classifies VCTK speakers at 84% — external validation that the dominant binary structure in §4.1 is gender, resolving a limitation the first revision could only flag (§5).

Figure A2 shows the same story geometrically: per-layer discriminant point clouds pull apart at view-specific depths (language slowly, over twenty layers; token-conditioned emotion almost immediately) and begin to re-merge near the output.

4.2 Two Routes, One (Coarse) Latent

Re-deriving the capture path while writing this paper revealed that the carrier's audio was never loaded: the loop passed the emotional clip's audio to both conditions, so the as-captured contrast is audio held constant, ±one token — accidentally a cleaner isolation of the token's marginal effect than the design. The two conditions share their audio and differ by one prepended token (plus, for roughly half the clips, which of RAVDESS's two fixed sentences serves as the reference transcript — a near-no-op), yet differ in every readability measure (Figure A3a): on the original split, peak probe accuracy 96.7% (layer 11) with the token vs. 76.7% (layer 22) without (CV: 98.3% vs. 61.7%), and peak Fisher separation 0.756 (layer 20) vs. 0.260 (layer 6) — roughly 3×. Each <|emotion:·|> tag is a single dedicated vocabulary token, an almost maximally clean conditioning signal; prepending it to a recording the model already hears still transforms how legibly the emotion is encoded. Hearing leaves affect smeared across the stack; being told snaps it into a linearly readable code.

Figure A3b asks where the token's contribution goes. For each emotion we compare the direction the residual stream moves (relative to neutral) with and without the token; since the audio is shared, vtagE = vvoiceE + δE where δE is the token's marginal displacement. The two directions align: angry at cosine 0.9755 at layer 6 (≈12.7° in a 2560-dim space), happy at 0.9273 (layer 5), surprise at 0.9205 (layer 6); the mean over all layers and emotions is 0.874 (the layer-6 mean is 0.905). And the contribution is large: the token-augmented mean-offset norm exceeds the voice-only one for every emotion at layer 18 (ratios 1.18–1.92 in the all-clip construction; the originally published 18-clip derive means gave 1.38–2.55). The token does not open a second pathway; it reinforces, at roughly double strength, the very direction heard affect already writes.

Two qualifications. First, because vtagE = vvoiceE + δE by construction, part of the alignment is inherited; the interpretable signal is the contrast across emotions and layers, not the absolute level. Sad sits lowest here too (never above 0.811, with δ slightly anti-aligned, cos(δ, vvoice) = −0.23 at layer 6 vs. +0.66 to +0.76 for the others) — a pattern the reliability analysis below re-attributes to sad's weak, noisy direction rather than to genuine disagreement. Second, both conditions use the same underlying RAVDESS material (a within-dataset contrast).

The told-only condition, and convergence without shared audio. The corrected re-capture gives the told-only cell of Table A2. Two results. (i) Readability: told-only reads at 99.5% [96.7–100] at layer 20, 97.5% already by layer 11, and a full 100% under speaker-disjoint CV — statistically indistinguishable from voice+token (one discordant clip; §4.5). Emotional audio neither helps nor hurts a clean discrete instruction; the 2×2's real structure is one bit, told vs. not told. (ii) Geometry: told-only directions share no audio with the voice-only directions, removing the vtag = vvoice + δ construction entirely — and they still converge: angry 0.902 (layer 6), happy 0.864 (layer 4), surprise 0.859 (layer 6), Table A3. Two genuinely independent input routes — a discrete vocabulary token and heard prosody — write overlapping internal directions.

Calibrating the cosines — the convergence is real but coarse. The revision subjects these cosines to the checks they previously lacked (Figure A4). First, the honest zero point: random same-size-subset diff-of-means pairs from these very datasets give mean |cos| ≈ 0.23 at layer 6 (anisotropy-matched null; the isotropic floor at D=2560 is 0.016), so raw cosines of 0.86–0.90 clear the null decisively — but the null is fourteen times higher than naive intuition. Second, specificity: the full voice×told matrix has a large off-diagonal — happy-voice aligns with angry-told at 0.926, above its own diagonal 0.863; the four-emotion diagonal mean beats the off-diagonal mean only modestly (0.77 vs. 0.68 at layer 6). Third, the sex-composition confound: the emotional draws are 6M/14F against neutral's 11M/9F, and gender is the model's single most readable direction; projecting each dataset's own gender direction out of both sides (six-emotion analysis) drops angry from 0.902 to 0.73 (happy 0.86→0.69, surprise 0.86→0.65), and sex-balanced bootstrap resampling gives compatible values. The deepest version of the finding: the six heard-emotion directions are themselves mutually aligned at 0.72–0.96 at layer 6 — the layer-6 "emotion code" is essentially one emotional-expressiveness direction (relative to neutral) with small per-emotion tilts. The honest summary replaces "two mechanisms, one latent": the token and the voice converge on a shared, coarse expressive direction — dominated by emotionality-vs-neutral, partly gender-shaped — while the per-emotion identity that probes read near-perfectly is a much smaller component on which the two interfaces agree only partially (diagonal − off-diagonal gap ≈0.04–0.08).

"Sad" was an artifact of unreliable directions, not a real exception. The earlier release headlined sad's low independent-audio convergence (0.445) as a per-emotion dissociation. Split-half reliability dissolves it: sad's own direction reproduces across disjoint clip halves at only 0.36 (voice) and 0.17 (told) at layer 6 — against 0.51–0.93 for the other emotions — and the attenuation-corrected convergence (obs/√(rarb)) is consistent with full alignment up to noise (corrected ≥ 1 at every key layer). The same correction leaves the other emotions essentially at their raw values (Table A3). What is real about sad is that its direction is weak — the model moves little and inconsistently for sadness at n=20 — not that its two interfaces disagree. We retract the earlier "exception" framing.

Six emotions, same story — and a refuted prediction. A second capture extends both conditions with fearful and disgust (both single native tokens; same 15 RAVDESS actors). Both converge across routes (fearful raw 0.76, reliability-corrected 1.16; disgust raw 0.88, corrected 1.11 at layer 6), and the 6×6 voice×told matrix keeps the same signature — diagonal mean 0.78 vs. off-diagonal 0.74 at layer 6. We pre-registered (in the capture config) the prediction that a valence/arousal organization would put low-arousal disgust nearer sad; it does not — disgust patterns with the strong emotions, and the ordering across emotions tracks direction reliability, not affect category. Convergence between hearing and telling is thus a robust, general property of this model's emotion handling; per-emotion selectivity of the shared code is weak everywhere, and no affect category is a special case once reliability is accounted for.

4.3 Geometry: Non-Collinear Languages, Orthogonal Views

Languages own separate directions mid-stack. Measured at every layer: across layers 8–28 the worst of the 15 pairwise cosines among the six language one-vs-rest directions peaks at |cos| ≈ 0.51 (at layer 18 it is 0.446; e.g. en·zh −0.007, ko·ja +0.032), with norms 35–51 at layer 18. This non-collinearity is a synthesis-conditioned property: comparable vectors derived from text-stream proxies in earlier work on the same model were near-collinear (cos ≈ 0.99).

The deepest layers explode. By layer 34 the same construction yields norms of 363–730 (≈13–19× the layer-12 values) and the worst pairwise cosine rises to 0.66, with sign flips in several pairs (en·ko −0.234 → +0.158; de·zh → +0.567). Combined with the late-stack decay of probe accuracy and Fisher separation, we read layers 34–35 as reorganizing representations for token emission rather than maintaining attribute geometry. (The original release sampled geometry at layers 12/18/34; the revision computes it at all 36 layers.)

Language ⊥ gender, at every layer. Across all 36 layers, the mean |cos| between the six language directions and the gender direction stays within [0.113, 0.215] (raw diff-of-means vectors; minimum at layer 19). Projecting the gender direction out of the language directions preserves ≥ 97.0% of their norm at every depth. (The originally published layer-20 value, 0.165, used τ-thresholded vectors; the raw-vector value there is 0.118, ≈83°.) To the extent voice cloning is compositional — any language in any voice — this near-orthogonality is the geometry one would expect to find, and does, everywhere in the stack.

All pairs, not one. The second revision extends the measurement to every attribute pair (full-stack one-vs-rest directions, all 36 layers; the full-stack language×gender range, [0.128, 0.284], sits slightly above the derive-split estimate quoted above). Most pairs are comparably near-orthogonal — language×accent [0.06, 0.15], accent×told-emotion [0.04, 0.13], language×told-emotion [0.08, 0.35] — with one instructive exception: gender×accent reaches [0.16, 0.33], the least orthogonal pair measured. That is exactly where the corpus is confounded: our American accent group is all-female (§4.4), and the geometry sees it. Cross-corpus caveat: these directions come from different corpora and carriers, so the numbers measure subspace alignment, not within-distribution interaction.

4.4 The 100% We Declined to Headline — Measured

The accent probe reads 100% at every layer from 11 onward (in-split CV: 99.5% [96–100]). We flagged it as a diagnostic, not a finding: the corpus mirror provides only 3 speakers per accent, shared between train and test splits, so a probe can succeed by identifying who is speaking rather than how. The signature was in the shape: 93.2% already at layer 0 (CV), and the flattest Fisher profile of any view (range 0.156–0.221 — no consolidation depth).

The revision tests the confound directly with leave-one-speaker-out evaluation: train on two speakers per accent, test on the held-out third (three folds). Speaker-disjoint accuracy collapses to 67.7% at layer 20 (chance 25%; 41.7% at layer 0) — and, tellingly, the collapse restores the ladder's shape: flat and modest through the early stack, then a genuine peak at layers 19–21, exactly where language consolidates. The second revision then disciplines this number too, since 67.7% is a max over 36 layers (winner's curse): at the layer the in-split CV itself selects (22), LOSO reads 54.2%; the pre-specified L18–22 window mean is 61.0%; and a speaker-level permutation null (accent labels shuffled across the 12 speakers, max over the same window, 100 draws) puts its 97.5th percentile at 53.0%. The speaker-independent accent signal is real — the window mean clears the null, and the acoustic-feature baseline manages only 34.4% under the same LOSO (§4.5) — but "real and modest" means the window mean, not the layer-max. The honest accent story is therefore two findings: most of the 100% was speaker identity, and the speaker-independent remainder is real, modest, and mid-stack. Speaker-disjoint evaluation is the default protocol in paralinguistics [5]; our addition for the layer-resolved probing setting — where small-N convenience corpora make speaker leakage easy to miss — is the shape diagnostic: a probe that is flat-high from layer 0 is reading identity, and a collapse that restores depth structure marks the content that remains. A further caveat the revision surfaced: our American accent group is all-female (official VCTK metadata), so the in-split accent probe could also lean on gender; the LOSO number inherits this imbalance.

4.5 Validity: Baselines, Sensitivity, and What the Numbers Can Bear

Acoustic-feature baselines — is the ladder in the model or in the stimulus? A 39-dimensional model-free feature vector (13 MFCC means + stds, F0 statistics, spectral centroid/rolloff/flux, ZCR, log-energy) from each view's actual input audio, probed with the identical CV protocol, separates stimulus from computation. Language: acoustics 79.7% vs. the model 98.9% — MFCCs carry much of language identity, the model adds the rest. Gender: 70.8% vs. 99.2%. Heard emotion: 34.6% (actor-disjoint 31.7%) vs. 61.7% — the model roughly doubles what shallow acoustics extract, so the 61.7% is not trivially "in the input". Accent is the sharpest lesson: acoustics score 80.7% on speaker-overlapping splits but 34.4% under LOSO — shallow features carry almost no speaker-independent accent information, which makes the model's 67.7% LOSO peak (against its own speaker-permutation null; §4.4) a genuine model contribution. The told-only condition provides the designed control: its input audio is a neutral carrier, and acoustics predict the tag at 8.8% — at/below chance — while the model's residuals read it at 99.5%. The token's meaning is computed, not heard.

Real confidence intervals, and which ordering claims survive. The repeated-split bands report split-to-split variability, not clip-sampling uncertainty; pooled 5-fold CV (each clip tested exactly once) gives exact binomial CIs at the peak layers: language 99.2% [98.1, 99.7] (n=600), gender 100% [91.2, 100] (n=40), heard emotion 58.0% [47.7, 67.8] (n=100), voice+token 99.0% [94.6, 100], told-only 100% [96.4, 100], accent (in-split) 100% [95.5, 100]. Paired exact McNemar tests on the pooled predictions settle the orderings: told-only vs. voice+token differ on exactly one clip (p=1.0) — the earlier ranking of told-only as the single most readable condition is not statistically supported and we withdraw it; both token conditions beat voice-only on 41–42 discordant clips with zero reversals (p<10⁻⁹). The 2×2's real structure is one bit: told vs. not told.

Probe-choice sensitivity. Across PCA k ∈ {16, 32, 64, 128} and ridge λ ∈ {0.1, 1, 10} at each axis's peak layer, λ is irrelevant (largest change from any λ swap: 6×10⁻⁴; PCA whitening) and k matters only at the low end (k=16 costs language 17 and heard emotion 10 points; k ∈ {64, 128} agree within 0.2 points on every axis). A probe-free nearest-centroid classifier preserves every qualitative claim (told-only 89.3%, accent 81.3%, language 74.9%, heard emotion 47.8%): the ladder's order is not an artifact of the PCA(64)+ridge choice.

Learning curves — ceiling or data starvation? Told-only saturates (92.3% at 4 training clips/class → 99.5% at 14). Heard emotion is still rising at our maximum (43.6% at 4 → 62.5% at 14, layer 22): 61.7% is a lower bound on heard-emotion readability, not a ceiling — a larger corpus would raise it, and claims about "the hardest listening task" are claims about our operating point.

5 Limitations

One model, one checkpoint, laptop-scale data. Cross-validation replaced all single-split claims and cost us one: voice-only emotion fell from 76.7% (single split) to a CV mean of 61.7% [46.7–76.7] — the original number was the top of its own band. Gender labels are taken from FLEURS metadata at face value, and gender's permutation null is bimodal (97.5th percentile up to 0.917 across layers): with two tight clusters, any random binary labeling of the training set is either right or backwards, so within-corpus accuracy alone cannot distinguish "gender" from "some dominant binary structure". The second revision resolves this externally: the FLEURS-trained probe (and even the raw direction's projection sign) classifies VCTK speakers of known metadata gender at 98.8% / 84% (§4.1) — the dominant binary structure is gender. The η² values in Figure A2 are in-sample and inflated at small N; we use them for shape only.

The carrier-audio discovery is narrated once, as method, in §4.2; what remains here is its residue: all voice+token results are stated for the as-captured audio-held-constant contrast, and the earlier project report's "tag on a neutral voice" description of that run is superseded.

Further: the language residuals are reused from a monolingual-production capture of the same model rather than a fresh sweep; each condition is a single seeded capture run (generation is hardware-stochastic on MPS bf16, so re-capture reproduces distributions, not bytes); the emotion corpus is acted, North-American English, two fixed sentences — ecological validity for spontaneous or non-English affect is unknown; the emotional-clip draw is female-skewed (6 male / 14 female clips per emotion vs. the corpus's balanced actors) — now measured as a confound and controlled in Table A3, not merely disclosed; and every measurement mean-pools the residual stream over the generated sequence, so token-position dynamics (when in generation an attribute becomes readable) are invisible to this instrument. Construction details worth knowing: the published shared-audio convergence curves (mac22) use derive-split means (18 clips) while the revision's δ analysis uses all 20 — the two agree within 0.046 everywhere; and the originally published orthogonality value used τ-thresholded vectors where the revision uses raw ones (0.165 vs. 0.118 at layer 20). And all results are correlational maps of what layers encode; causal use of these directions is a separate question, studied elsewhere with intervention methods and not claimed here.

6 Conclusion

Read passively, layer by layer, a voice-cloning audio LLM organizes the attributes of a voice on a ladder: identity-like cues (gender, speaker) are linearly present near the input and consolidate mid-stack; language assembles into a mid-network plateau before the output layers dissolve it; and emotion divides by interface — hearing alone averages 61.7% at its best layer, while the model's own token, with or without any emotional audio underneath, is near-perfectly readable (98–99.5%, and 100% actor-disjoint). The two interfaces write overlapping directions even with fully independent audio, but the calibration this revision adds shows the shared latent is coarse — essentially one emotional-expressiveness direction with weak per-emotion selectivity — and that the one apparent per-emotion dissociation (sad) was an artifact of unreliable small-n directions. The views the model must recombine at will — language and gender — sit in near-orthogonal subspaces. The map is small-N and observational, but its structure (ordered depths, calibrated route convergence, orthogonal views, cross-corpus transfer, and two honestly-measured artifacts) offers a compact target for the causal question that naturally follows: does the model use what its layers so legibly encode?

Impact Statement

This is a read-only interpretability study of a publicly released voice-cloning model: we add no synthesis capability, release no cloned audio of real identities beyond corpus samples used under their licenses, and modify no weights. Voice cloning itself is dual-use — impersonation and fraud are the salient risks — and mapping where attribute information lives inside such models is, in our judgment, defense-relevant: the same maps that explain how a cloner represents speaker identity are the natural starting point for watermarking, attribution, and detection work. We do not publish steering recipes here; the intervention-based companion study is deliberately excluded.
